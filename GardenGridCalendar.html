Januarymidsummer
Prune
  • Roses — deadhead spent blooms regularly to encourage rebloom
Protect
  • Indian rhododendron — mulch roots to keep cool
  • Elephant bush — watch for sunscorch; shade cloth if >35°C
  • Gardenia — avoid midday sun stress
  • White cedars — check windward side for browning; ensure adequate mulch at base
Mulch
  • Top up mulch across all beds to conserve summer moisture
Water
  • All beds — water deeply 2–3×/week in heat
  • Liquidambar, birches, magnolia cucumber — don't let roots dry out
  • White cedars — deep water if prolonged heat; established plants drought-tolerant but can brown in hot dry wind
  • Newly planted acacia silver — keep consistently moist
Februarylate summer
Prune
  • Hydrangeas — prune now, immediately after flowering, cutting flowering shoots back to the next healthy bud. Do not leave until autumn or winter — next season's buds form from late summer onwards
  • White cedars — light shaping trim after main summer flush if needed. Never cut back into bare brown wood — it will not reshoot
Feed
  • Roses — potassium-rich feed for late summer/autumn flush
Protect
  • Watch for powdery mildew on roses and hydrangeas in humid spells
Water
  • Continue deep watering 2–3×/week
  • Camellias — keep consistently moist as buds begin to set
Marchearly autumn
Prune
  • Roses — trim for autumn flush; cut to outward-facing buds
  • Agapanthus — deadhead spent flower stalks
  • Weigela — remove dead/weak stems after flowering
  • Hydrangeas — complete any remaining deadheading if not done in February
Feed
  • Camellias — apply camellia/azalea fertiliser (spring and autumn are the two key feeds)
  • Azaleas & rhododendrons — feed after flowering with azalea food
  • Gardenias — final feed before growth slows
Plant / divide
  • Cool-season annuals (violas, forget-me-nots) — sow or plant out
  • Divide agapanthus clumps if overcrowded
Water
  • Begin reducing watering frequency as temperatures ease
Aprilmid-autumn
Prune
  • Climbing roses — tie in new canes, remove dead wood
  • Liquidambar — shape while still in leaf
Feed
  • Japanese maples — slow-release fertiliser
  • Birches — general fertiliser before dormancy
  • Protea — low-phosphorus native fertiliser only if in very poor/sandy soil; established garden plants generally don't need feeding
  • Grevillea — low-phosphorus native fertiliser (phosphorus below 3%)
Plant / divide
  • Bulbs (tulips, daffodils, ranunculus) — plant now for spring
  • Camellias — excellent time to plant new ones
  • White cedars — autumn is a good planting time; allows root establishment before summer
Mulch
  • Top up mulch ahead of winter — 7–10cm across beds
Maylate autumn
Prune
  • Japanese maples — prune for shape as leaves drop
  • Cherry plum & plum — begin dormant pruning
  • Meadowsweet (Spiraea) — cut back hard after flowering
Feed
  • Deciduous trees — last feed as dormancy approaches
Plant / divide
  • Bare-root roses — plant now through July
  • Bare-root deciduous trees — ideal planting window
Protect
  • Elephant bush — move to most sheltered spot; frost risk begins
  • Bird of paradise — mulch crown well
  • Gardenia — protect from cold winds
Mulch
  • Mulch around frost-sensitive plants
Juneearly winter
Prune
  • Crape myrtle — prune hard for shape now
  • Cherry yoshino & Japanese cherry — prune dormant stone fruits
Plant / divide
  • Bare-root roses — continue planting
  • Bare-root fruit trees (plum, cherry plum)
Protect
  • Gardenia — frost cloth on cold nights
  • Elephant bush — frost cloth or bring under cover
  • Bird of paradise — straw mulch around crown
  • Silver ponysfoot — may die back; will reshoot in spring
  • White cedars — no protection needed; fully frost hardy at your elevation
Water
  • Reduce watering — rain generally sufficient; check newly planted specimens
Frost risk at your elevation — check forecasts
Julymidwinter
Prune
  • Plum & cherry plum — complete dormant pruning
  • Roses — do NOT prune yet at your elevation; wait until August when frost risk eases (new growth after pruning is vulnerable to frost damage)
Plant / divide
  • Bare-root roses and trees — final month
Protect
  • Continue monitoring frost-sensitive plants
  • Gardenia & elephant bush — maintain frost protection
Frost risk at your elevation — check forecasts
Augustlate winter
Prune
  • Roses — main winter prune this month (correct timing for your frost-prone elevation). Cut to outward-facing buds, remove crossing canes and dead wood. Spray with lime sulphur after pruning
  • Camellias — prune for shape now, before new spring growth begins. This is the correct window — pruning later risks removing next season's flower buds
  • Grevillea — light tip prune to encourage bushy growth
  • White cedars — light shaping prune before new spring growth begins. Never cut into bare brown wood
Feed
  • Roses — apply rose food after pruning
  • Camellias — feed with camellia/azalea fertiliser after pruning
  • Azaleas & rhododendrons — begin feeding as buds swell
  • White cedars — apply balanced slow-release fertiliser now as growth resumes; light feeders so don't overdo it
Plant / divide
  • Camellias — repot container specimens now (August–September is ideal)
Protect
  • Watch for late frosts — August can still deliver cold nights at your elevation
Water
  • Increase watering gradually as growth resumes
Frost risk at your elevation — check forecasts
Septemberearly spring
Prune
  • Indian hawthorn — light prune after flowering
  • Mock orange (Philadelphus) — remove oldest stems after flowering (flowers on old wood — always prune after, not before)
  • Port wine magnolia — light shape only
Feed
  • Roses — begin regular fortnightly feeding program
  • Camellias — continue spring feed
  • Liquidambar & maples — slow-release fertiliser
  • Gardenias — begin feeding with acid fertiliser
  • Grevillea — spring feed with low-phosphorus native fertiliser
Plant / divide
  • Warm-season seedlings — sow indoors
  • Divide violets and forget-me-nots
Protect
  • Last frost risk — keep cloth handy for cold snaps
Water
  • Increase frequency as growth accelerates
Octobermid-spring
Prune
  • Weigela — prune immediately after flowering
  • Azaleas & rhododendrons — shape lightly after flowering (flower on old wood — always prune after, not before)
  • Protea — prune flowered stems only, cutting back to ~10cm of healthy stem. Leave all unflowered stems untouched — these carry next year's blooms
Feed
  • Roses — fortnightly feed continues
  • Hydrangeas — begin feeding for summer blooms
  • Japanese maple — liquid feed monthly
  • Acacia silver — low-phosphorus native fertiliser
Plant / divide
  • Warm-season annuals — plant out after frost risk passes
  • Star jasmine — good time to plant climbers
Water
  • Increase watering as temperatures rise
Novemberlate spring
Prune
  • Camellias — shape lightly after all flowers finish if needed
  • Climbing roses — tie in and direct new growth
  • Protea — only prune stems that flowered this season; never remove unflowered stems
Feed
  • Roses — continue fortnightly
  • Hydrangeas — potassium-rich feed to support flower colour
  • Bird of paradise — slow-release fertiliser
Plant / divide
  • Agapanthus — divide if overcrowded
  • Koromiko (Hebe) — good time to plant or transplant
Protect
  • Elephant bush — watch as heat returns
Mulch
  • Top up mulch before summer heat — aim for 7–10cm
Water
  • Deep water weekly — heat building
  • White cedars — water if prolonged dry spell; watch for wind-burn browning
Decemberearly summer
Prune
  • Roses — deadhead regularly to encourage rebloom
  • Liquidambar — avoid pruning (bleeds sap in active growth)
Feed
  • Roses — last feed mid-December; stop feeding in peak heat
  • Gardenias — feed if flowering well
Protect
  • Elephant bush — ensure good airflow; avoid wetting foliage
  • Magnolia — water young tree deeply and regularly
Mulch
  • Mulch all exposed beds to manage summer heat
Water
  • Deep water 2–3×/week
  • Camellias — keep moist as buds set for winter flowering
  • Agapanthus — drought-tolerant but better with regular water in heat
  • White cedars — deep water fortnightly if no significant rain
